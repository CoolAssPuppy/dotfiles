# Candidate interview analysis prompt

Use this prompt to evaluate a final-stage candidate interview transcript and produce a defensible hire or no-hire recommendation. The output should stand on its own so that a hiring manager who never read the transcript can make a confident decision from it.

**When to use:** After a final-stage candidate interview, when given a recording or transcript and asked to write up the assessment.

## Operating principles

- This is a values and judgment filter, not a skills test. By this stage, the candidate can almost certainly do the job.
- Default to no when uncertain. The cost of a bad hire dwarfs the cost of missing a good one.
- Apply the airport test. Would you be excited to spend a week with this person at an offsite. If the honest answer is "glad I would not have to interface with them daily," it is a no.
- The bar is better than the median teammate. If they would not raise the average, pass.
- Score on demonstrated behavior, not on values language or polish.

## The six values to score against

1. Egoless. Mission first, separates self-worth from being right.
2. Truth seeking. Intellectually honest, updates beliefs on evidence.
3. Kaizen mindset. Continuous, iterative improvement.
4. Batteries included. High agency, does not wait to be asked.
5. Undeniable. Reliable, gets it done, earns external trust.
6. Willing to do the schlep. Builds the playbook, not just runs it.

## Step 1. Load context

- Read the transcript end to end before scoring anything.
- Note the candidate name, the role, and any prior interview feedback or resume context provided.
- Read the resume bottom up. Look at progression and transitions, not just the latest title.

## Step 2. Extract the candidate's full narrative

Write a two to four sentence summary of the candidate as a story, not a checklist. Include:

- The arc of their career and what their transitions reveal about motivation.
- Their stated reason for considering this role.
- The texture of how they speak: relaxed and offering information freely, or tight and answering only what is asked.

## Step 3. Score the six values

For each value, rate Strong, Mixed, Weak, or Not Assessed. Quote the specific transcript passage that supports the rating. Do not score on values language, score on demonstrated behavior.

- Egoless. Did they own a wrong call without justification? Did they give credit unprompted?
- Truth seeking. Did they update a belief based on evidence? Did they describe an opinion that got more complicated, not simpler?
- Kaizen mindset. Are they currently iterating on something? Did they describe a failed improvement attempt?
- Batteries included. Did they fix something unowned? Did they act before alignment?
- Undeniable. Is reliability described by others' dependence, not self-promotion?
- Willing to do the schlep. Did they describe the unglamorous, in-the-trenches work, or only the high-level coordination?

Mark a value Not Assessed rather than guessing. Missing signal is information.

## Step 4. Run the three orange flag checks

For each archetype, name which side the candidate falls on and quote the evidence.

- Tourist versus Owner. Are they more excited about what the company offers them (remote, equity, brand) or about a problem they would own?
- Corporate Executor versus Schlepper. When asked about their most successful project, did they describe a CSV they cleaned, a pipeline they wrote, a doc they rebuilt? Or did they describe alignment, coordination, and process?
- Scene Hopper versus Egoless Peer. Do their transitions follow what is hot, or do they describe staying through friction because they cared about the team?

## Step 5. Identify patterns, not anecdotes

A single example is a story. Two or more is a pattern. Call out:

- Repeated friction with teams or managers across roles.
- Repeated language that externalizes blame ("unclear requirements," "the org was misaligned").
- Repeated learning loops where they took feedback, updated, and changed behavior.
- Repeated initiative without permission.

## Step 6. The airport test

Answer in one sentence: would I be excited to spend a week with this person at an offsite, or would I be quietly relieved we are remote? If the answer is the latter, the recommendation is no, regardless of skill.

## Step 7. Produce the feedback document

Write the feedback in this exact structure.

**Overall recommendation.** Strong Yes, Yes, No, or Strong No. One sentence.

**Performance expectation.** Top 1%, Top 10%, Top 50%, or Outside Top 50% versus the existing team.

**Values alignment.** A short list of the six values with Strong, Mixed, Weak, or Not Assessed for each.

**Assessment.** Two or three short paragraphs. Lead with the dominant pattern, support with two to three quoted moments from the transcript, then connect those behaviors to specific values. No vague language. No buzzwords. Someone reading this alone should be able to make the call.

**Risk areas and orange flags.** Specific, evidenced. Tied to one of the three archetypes if relevant.

**Growth focus area** (if Yes or Strong Yes) or **Critical growth area** (if No or Strong No). Name the single most important development edge or the single primary reason for the no.

**Airport test.** One sentence. Honest.

## Self-check before finalizing

- Did I quote the transcript directly at least three times?
- Did I show patterns across multiple moments, not just one anecdote?
- Did I tie behavior to specific values, not generic praise?
- Could a hiring manager who never read the transcript make a confident decision from this alone?
- If on the fence, did I default to no?
