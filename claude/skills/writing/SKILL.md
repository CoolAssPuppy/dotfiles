# Writing voice guide

This is the authoritative source for voice, tone, banned phrases, and writing rules across all contexts. When in doubt, this file wins.

## Fiction voice

Derived from analysis of The Midnight Coder's Children and related work.

### What defines it

- **Opens with action, not setup.** Drop readers into a moment. No stage-setting. No "It was a Tuesday in September."
- **Specific timestamps and details.** "5:43am" not "early morning." "Badge number 4471" not "her badge." Precision signals a character who notices everything.
- **Short sentences for tension.** "Failure." as its own beat. One-word paragraphs when the moment demands it. Then expand into longer sentences for breathing room.
- **Physical grounding for emotion.** Characters don't feel sad. They stare at a crack in the ceiling. They press their thumb into their palm until the nail leaves a mark. The body tells the truth the mind won't say.
- **Characters defined through habit and precision.** What someone does at 5:43am on a Saturday tells you who they are. Routines reveal character. Disrupted routines reveal stakes.
- **Emotion shown through physical tells.** Looking down. Throat clearing. Hands going still. The moment someone stops fidgeting is the moment they're truly afraid.
- **Internal thought in italics, kept brief.** One line. Maybe two. Never a paragraph of interiority. The reader fills in the rest.
- **No exposition dumps.** Weave technical detail into character action. The reader learns about database architecture by watching someone work, not by reading a textbook insert.
- **Sentence variation as rhythm.** Long sentence with multiple clauses building momentum and stacking detail. Then short. Then medium. Like breathing.
- **Lists of three for rhythm.** "She checked the monitors, verified the timestamps, and reached for her coffee." Three beats. Never four. Rarely two.
- **Secondary characters via single telling details.** One physical trait. One habit. One line of dialogue. That's enough for a minor character to live.
- **Restrained metaphors.** One per scene maximum. Make it count. Connect it to the story's world (technology, cuisine, astronomy). Never strain for cleverness.
- **Cultural specificity.** Food, language fragments, ritual. These aren't decoration. They're the geography of displacement, the bridge between the home you left and the home that won't fully have you.
- **Dialogue as character revelation through what is NOT said.** The pause. The subject change. The question that answers a different question. People reveal themselves by what they avoid.

### BAD fiction

> She felt overwhelmed by the weight of her responsibilities. The pressure was immense, and she didn't know how she could possibly handle everything on her plate. It was like carrying the world on her shoulders.

### GOOD fiction

> She pressed her badge to the reader at 5:43am, same as every Saturday for eleven years. The lab was dark except for the glow of seventeen monitors, each cycling through feeds she'd memorized the way other mothers memorized lullabies.

## Non-fiction voice

Derived from analysis of Picks and Shovels and blog work.

### What defines it

- **Conversational authority.** You've done this. You know the terrain. But you're not lecturing. You're the experienced colleague at the next desk, leaning over to say "here's what actually works."
- **Personal anecdotes woven naturally.** Not "Let me tell you a story." Just a moment dropped in. "When we launched Supabase's vector feature, the first thing that broke was..." Concrete. Specific. In service of the point.
- **Direct address.** "You probably feel" not "one might feel." "Your team" not "teams in general." The reader is a person, not an abstraction.
- **States opinions without hedging.** "This doesn't work" not "this might not be the most optimal approach." Conviction earns trust. Wishy-washy earns nothing.
- **Defines terms without condescension.** "RLS (Row Level Security) lets you set database-level rules about who sees what" not "RLS, which stands for Row Level Security and is a feature of PostgreSQL that many developers may not be familiar with..."
- **Historical context dropped casually.** "Developers have been writing documentation since before there were developers to read it." One sentence. No footnote. Trust the reader.
- **Dry, understated humor.** Never a joke that stops the text. A wry observation. A self-aware aside. Humor that works because it's true, not because it's trying.
- **Structure-heavy with clean section breaks.** Headers do work. Bullets list specifics. Paragraphs explain concepts. Balance all three.
- **Philosophy distilled to memorable phrases.** "Help First." "Sell the picks and shovels." Short enough to remember. True enough to repeat.
- **Specific examples over abstractions.** Not "companies that do developer marketing well" but "Stripe's API documentation redesign in 2018."
- **Acknowledges nuance.** "Metrics tell an incomplete story" not "metrics are dead." The world is complicated. Pretending otherwise insults the reader.
- **Self-deprecating where earned.** "I got this wrong for three years before I figured it out." Vulnerability in service of credibility, not performance.

### BAD non-fiction

> In today's rapidly evolving landscape, it's crucial to leverage cutting-edge strategies to unlock developer engagement. By harnessing the power of community-driven growth, you can revolutionize your approach to technical marketing.

### GOOD non-fiction

> Most developer marketing advice boils down to "write good docs and go to conferences." That's not wrong. It's just not enough. Here's what the advice leaves out.

## Banned words and phrases

This is the single authoritative list. If it's here, it doesn't appear in any content.

### Banned words

- Delve
- Dive into (and all variants: deep dive, diving into, let's dive)
- Unpack
- Harness
- Leverage (as a verb)
- Utilize (use "use")
- Game-changer
- Cutting-edge
- Revolutionary
- Unlock (as metaphor: "unlock growth," "unlock potential")
- Landscape (as in "the current landscape")
- Ecosystem (unless literally referring to a biological or software ecosystem with named components)
- Robust
- Seamless
- Streamline
- Elevate
- Empower
- Navigate (as metaphor: "navigate challenges")
- Reimagine
- Supercharge
- Synergy
- Holistic
- Paradigm
- Disrupt/disruptive (unless quoting someone else)
- Innovative (show it, don't label it)

### Banned constructions (FATAL)

These are never acceptable. Their presence means the text needs rewriting.

- "This isn't X. This is Y." (contrarian flip. Always.)
- "It's not X, it's Y." (same pattern, different punctuation.)
- "of someone who" ("the hands of someone who...")
- "the kind of person who" ("she was the kind of person who...")

### Banned openers

- "I've spent [number] years..." (false authority opener. Earn authority through insight, not resume.)
- "Everyone is talking about... Everyone is wrong..." (contrarian bait. Cheap engagement trick.)
- "In today's [adjective] world/landscape/era..." (throat-clearing.)
- "Let me tell you a story..." (just tell the story.)
- "What if I told you..." (clickbait.)

### Banned from Wikipedia AI writing guide

All phrases flagged on the [Wikipedia Signs of AI Writing Guide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Signs_of_AI_writing). This includes but is not limited to:

- "It's important to note that..."
- "It's worth mentioning..."
- "In conclusion..."
- "Furthermore..." / "Moreover..."
- "Firstly... Secondly... Thirdly..."
- "In the realm of..."
- "A testament to..."
- "Plays a crucial role"
- "Serves as a reminder"
- "A tapestry of..."
- "Resonates with..."
- "The intricacies of..."

## Writing rules with examples

### Get to the point. No throat-clearing.

BAD:
> Before we begin exploring the fascinating world of developer marketing, it's important to understand the context in which these strategies operate. The tech industry has undergone significant changes in recent years, and with these changes come new challenges and opportunities for marketers.

GOOD:
> Developer marketing has one job: help developers succeed with your product. Everything else is noise.

### Vary sentence length. Mix short and long.

BAD:
> The team analyzed the metrics. The team found problems. The team proposed solutions. The team implemented changes. The team measured results.

Also BAD:
> After conducting a comprehensive analysis of the available metrics across all deployment environments, the cross-functional team identified several critical issues that required immediate attention and subsequently proposed a series of targeted solutions designed to address the root causes of the observed performance degradation.

GOOD:
> The team spent three weeks analyzing metrics across every deployment environment. They found the problem in twelve minutes. A single misconfigured cache header was adding 400ms to every API call. The fix was one line.

### When uncertain, say so. Hedging is human.

BAD:
> This approach is definitively the best way to handle authentication in every scenario.

Also BAD:
> This approach might potentially be somewhat useful in certain situations depending on various factors.

GOOD:
> This approach works well for most B2B applications. For consumer apps with millions of users, you'll want to benchmark it against token-based auth first.

### Be specific. Use numbers, names, details.

BAD:
> A major tech company recently improved their developer experience significantly.

GOOD:
> Stripe reduced their API integration time from 7 days to 7 minutes by rewriting their onboarding flow in 2018.

### Never pad output to seem more thorough.

BAD:
> In summary, to summarize the key points we've discussed throughout this comprehensive guide, the main takeaways that you should remember and keep in mind as you move forward with your developer marketing strategy are as follows:

GOOD:
> Three things to remember: help first, measure what matters, and write docs like someone's production deployment depends on them. Because it does.

## Tone calibration by context

| Context | Register | Reading level | Humor | Hedging | Technical depth |
|---------|----------|---------------|-------|---------|-----------------|
| Professional books | Conversational authority | 6th grade | Dry, earned | Acknowledge nuance | Moderate with examples |
| Technical blog (Supabase) | Skilled colleague teaching | 5th-6th grade | Minimal, wry | Honest about trade-offs | High with working code |
| Personal blog | Relaxed, opinionated | 6th grade | More frequent, personal | Less hedging, more conviction | Moderate |
| Fiction | Elite craft, accessible | N/A | Character-driven | N/A | Woven into character action |
| Marketing course | Direct instruction | 6th grade | Occasional, self-deprecating | Nuanced but confident | Moderate with frameworks |

## How to match voice when revising

1. **Read the surrounding text first.** Match its rhythm, vocabulary, and sentence length distribution before writing anything.
2. **Keep original words wherever possible.** The goal is seamless integration, not replacement.
3. **Check for banned words/phrases after every revision.** Run a mental grep. If anything from the banned list crept in, rewrite.
4. **Test the "coffee conversation" standard.** Would you say this sentence out loud to a smart colleague? If it sounds stilted, rewrite.
5. **Count your metaphors.** One per major section in non-fiction. One per scene in fiction. If you've used more, cut the weakest ones.
6. **Verify the opening.** No throat-clearing. No false authority. Start with the point or start with action.
